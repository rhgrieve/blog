There's a particular quality to software that earns long-term use. It's not feature richness. It's not even simplicity in the reductive sense. It's something closer to *deference* — the tool knows when to recede.

I keep coming back to this when evaluating what to use and what to build. The best tools disappear into the work. They don't announce themselves. They don't demand configuration rituals. They don't need a community Discord to be usable.

## The test

Here's a rough heuristic: if you spend more time configuring the tool than using it in your first session, something has gone wrong. Not always — some tools are genuinely complex because the domain is complex. But for most things, the setup cost should be near zero.

## What this means for building

When I'm building something, I try to hold this standard. Every preference panel, every toggle, every "customizable" behavior is a small admission that I couldn't make a good default decision.

Sometimes that's honest. Sometimes it's a cop-out.

The tools I admire most made opinionated choices and stuck with them. They traded configurability for coherence. And that trade almost always pays off.
