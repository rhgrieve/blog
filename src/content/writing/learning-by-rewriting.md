I've never learned a technology by reading about it. Not really. I've learned things by building with them, breaking them, and rebuilding them.

There's a specific move that works better than anything else: take something that already exists, and rewrite it from scratch. Not to improve it. Just to understand it.

## The rewrite as a study tool

When you rewrite something, you're forced to answer every question the original author answered. Why this data structure? Why this order of operations? Why this API shape?

Reading the code, you can skip these questions. Your brain fills in plausible answers and moves on. But when you have to actually make the choices yourself, the gaps in your understanding become obvious.

## It doesn't have to be good

The rewrite doesn't need to be better than the original. It doesn't need to be complete. It's not a product — it's a study method. The value is in the process, not the artifact.

I've rewritten parts of tools I use daily — text editors, build systems, HTTP servers — not because I thought I could do better, but because I wanted to understand why they work the way they do.

## The compound effect

Each rewrite leaves you with a better mental model. Over time, these models compound. You start seeing patterns across domains. You develop intuition that can't come from reading alone.
