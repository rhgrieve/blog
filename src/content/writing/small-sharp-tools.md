The Unix philosophy is one of those ideas that keeps proving itself right. Not because it's clever, but because it maps onto how complexity actually works.

Small tools that do one thing. Clear interfaces between them. Composition over integration.

## Why it keeps winning

Every few years, someone builds a monolith that tries to do everything. It's impressive at first. Then it calcifies. The edges get weird. The abstractions leak. Eventually someone rewrites it as a collection of smaller things, and the cycle repeats.

The monolith fails because the world changes faster than any single system can adapt. Small tools can be swapped, rewired, abandoned individually. The system survives because no single part is load-bearing.

## Applied beyond software

I think about this pattern outside of code. A good daily routine is a composition of small, sharp habits — not a rigid monolithic schedule. A good team is a collection of people with clear roles and clean interfaces, not a single organism that thinks as one.

## The hard part

The hard part isn't building small tools. It's designing the interfaces between them. The protocol, the format, the contract. That's where the real work is — and it's the part most people skip.
